🦑 GIANT SQUID 🦑

Searching for a Sea Monster

By Mary M. Cerullo with Clyde F. E. Roper

A Simplified Interactive Story for Young Readers

🚢 A Creature of Giant Size 🚢

The mysterious giant squid!

Long, long ago, sailors told scary stories about the ocean. They said a huge monster lived deep in the water. The monster had long, twisting arms. It could grab sailors right off their ships!

The sailors called this creature the kraken. They said it was as big as an island. Its long arms could wrap around a whole ship and pull it down into the water.

Were these stories real? Or were they just made up?

🤔 Were the Stories Real?

Yes! The kraken was a real animal—the giant squid!

In 1874, a ship captain said he saw a giant sea monster. It tried to climb onto his ship! The captain told his men to shoot at it. Finally, the monster sank back into the ocean.

A writer named Jules Verne heard this story. He put it in his famous book. The book had a scary scene with a "squid of colossal size."

🔍 Early Clues 🔍

Sperm whale with mysterious round scars

After a while, people started finding real clues about these mysterious creatures.

In the 1700s and 1800s, ship captains hunted big whales called sperm whales. When they caught the whales, they noticed something strange. The whales had round scars all over their heads. Something had been fighting with these huge whales! But what?

Inside the whales' bellies, the captains found something else strange. They found big, sharp beaks. The beaks looked like bird beaks, but they were as big as a man's fist!

❓ What Do You Think?

Did sperm whales eat birds?

The captains figured it out. The beaks came from squids—GIANT squids!

Sailors knew about small squids. They had eaten them many times. Squids have suckers on their arms and a sharp beak in their mouth. But these beaks were HUGE. They could only come from a giant squid.

🎯 Battle Scars!

The round scars on the whales were as big as hockey pucks. They came from the giant squid's suckers fighting back against the whale!

🏠 Where Do Giant Squids Live? 🏠

Sometimes fishermen would catch a dead giant squid in their nets. Sometimes pieces of giant squid would wash up on the beach.

In 1873, fishermen in Canada caught what they called "a sea monster." They showed it to a church minister named Reverend Moses Harvey. He put the giant squid in his bathtub to show people! It was the first complete giant squid that scientists got to study.

Where Giant Squids Live

Giant squids live very, very deep in the ocean. They live about half a mile down—much deeper than people can swim or dive.

That's where they meet sperm whales. The whales dive down deep to find their favorite food—giant squid!

🌊 Why Do They Wash Up on Beaches?

Sometimes ocean currents change. The currents can bring deep-sea animals up closer to the surface. The water is warmer and different there. Sometimes this kills the deep-sea animals. That's why dead giant squids sometimes wash up on beaches.

👦 Meet the Squid Hunter 👦

Young Clyde following snail trails at the beach

A boy named Clyde Roper loved the ocean. When he was little, he would follow tiny snails along the beach. He watched them make trails in the sand.

Clyde didn't know it yet, but those snails were his first clue. Snails and squids are related! They're both soft-bodied animals called mollusks.

Some mollusks, like snails, have shells. Others, like squids, don't have shells. But squids have cool ways to protect themselves!

🚀 Squid Super Powers!

A squid can shoot water out of its body to zoom backward—like a rocket! 💨

A squid has 8 arms and 2 extra-long feeding tentacles. When it's hungry, the squid shoots out its tentacles to grab food. Then it pulls the food to its sharp beak.

🧠 Learning About Squids 🧠

When Clyde grew up, he went to college. He studied all kinds of squids and their relatives. There are octopuses, cuttlefishes, and even a creature called the chambered nautilus.

🐙 How Many Arms?

How many arms does an octopus have?

Octopuses have 8 arms. Squids have 8 arms plus 2 tentacles. But the chambered nautilus holds the record, with 80 to 100 arms!

All of these animals have big brains and big eyes. Their bodies hold their organs—hearts, stomach, and gills. (Most squids have THREE hearts!)

💪 Super Strong Beak!

The giant squid has suckers with small, sharp teeth that dig into its food. Its sharp beak is so strong, it can bite through steel cable!

Why Does the Whale Always Win?

The sperm whale's mouth is perfect for catching soft animals like squids. It has big teeth only on the bottom. Those huge teeth fit snugly into sockets on the top. This way the whale can hold on tight. A giant squid doesn't stand a chance!

🔬 Ocean Detective on the Chase 🔬

There are more than 500 kinds of squids in the ocean. Scientists named the giant squid "Architeuthis" (ark-i-TOOTH-iss). It means "the chief squid."

Clyde Roper decided he wanted to learn everything about giant squids. But how could he study an animal that no one could find? It was like being a detective looking for someone who had been hiding for hundreds of years!

🔍 How Scientists Solve Mysteries

1. They watch nature carefully and get ideas to test

2. They collect information and clues

3. Like detectives, they figure out what the clues mean

4. They never give up, even if it takes a long time!

Unlike TV detectives who can solve a case in an hour, it sometimes takes a lifetime—or longer—to solve a scientific mystery.

🐋 Looking for Clues in a Whale 🐋

Scientists examining a beached whale

One day when Clyde was in college, someone called about a dead sperm whale on the beach. Did anyone want to look at it? Yes! Clyde and his friends raced to the beach.

They jumped into a big hole that had been dug for the whale. They used knives and saws to cut into the whale's stomachs. (Sperm whales have THREE stomachs!) They reached deep inside, all the way up to their shoulders. It was a very messy, smelly job!

Hours later, they had found almost 1,000 squid beaks! That's how many squids one whale can eat! But none of the beaks were from giant squids.

However, the whale DID have big round scars around its mouth. This proved the whale had fought and eaten giant squids during its life.

🦈 Shark Alert!

Clyde and his friends were covered in blood from the whale. They wanted to wash off in the ocean. But then they saw something scary—dozens of shark fins in the water! The sharks smelled the whale blood. So the men had to drive home in their dirty, bloody clothes!

🎓 Becoming Dr. Roper 🎓

The Smithsonian Museum in Washington, D.C.

After many years of studying, Clyde became a doctor of science. People called him Dr. Roper. He got a job at a big museum in Washington, D.C. called the Smithsonian Institution.

At the museum, Dr. Roper studied all kinds of squids. Some were as small as his thumb. Others were almost as tall as he was!

People sent him squids from all over to study—including giant squids. But looking at dead squids only made Clyde more curious. He really, really wanted to see a LIVE giant squid swimming in the ocean.

🔢 How Many Giant Squids Are There?

Scientists have tried to count! They counted beaks in whale stomachs.

One sperm whale might eat 20,000 squids in one week! Only about 3 of those are giant squids.

There are at least 400,000 sperm whales in all the oceans. If you do the math, that means whales eat about 60 MILLION giant squids every year!

And not all giant squids get eaten by whales. So there might be many, many more millions hiding in the deep ocean! 🌊

👀 Amazing Eyes 👀

The giant squid's eye is as big as a car headlight!

The giant squid has the most amazing eyes. They are as big as car headlights—almost as big as Clyde's whole head! They're the biggest eyes in the whole world!

💡 Think About It!

Why would a giant squid need such HUGE eyes?

How do these eyes help the squid see in the dark, deep ocean? How far can they see? Clyde wanted to find out.

He talked to fishermen who had caught giant squids in their nets. He looked at squids that had washed up on shore. But dead squids couldn't answer his biggest question: How does a giant squid act when it's alive in the ocean?

Clyde decided he HAD to find out.

✨ Seeing in the Dark

Deep in the ocean, it's always dark. Below about 1,000 feet down, there's no sunlight at all.

But many ocean animals glow in the dark! They make their own light, like fireflies. 🔦

More than three-quarters of deep-sea animals can make light. Clyde wondered: Can giant squids glow in the dark too?

👹 Meeting the Red Devil 👹

The fierce Humboldt squid - "Red Devil"

Clyde couldn't swim with a giant squid yet. So he decided to meet the next best thing—a close relative of the giant squid.

He went to Mexico to see Humboldt squids. These squids can weigh 100 pounds and grow to be 8 feet long. That's not as big as giant squids, but they're still pretty big!

Humboldt squids have a scary reputation. At night, hundreds—even thousands—of them come up to hunt for fish and shrimp. Fishermen call them "diablo rojo"—red devil! The squid's body flashes bright red when it's caught. And it fights back hard! It will even attack people sometimes.

Clyde Gets Bitten!

One night, Clyde went out with fishermen and a film crew. They caught a big female Humboldt squid. Clyde put on his wetsuit and diving tank and jumped in the water to see her up close.

He was staring right into her open mouth when—SNAP! The squid bit him with her big beak and zoomed away!

After watching the squids for several hours, Clyde climbed back into the boat. He was very tired. When he took off his wetsuit, he saw blood! The squid had bitten him deep in his leg. He still has the scar today.

🤔 What Did Clyde Learn?

Watching the Humboldt squid helped Clyde imagine how a giant squid might hunt. It also made him wonder: Would a giant squid be as fierce as the red devil?

📹 Following the Whales 📹

A special camera attached to a whale!

Clyde learned a lot from studying the giant squid's cousins. Now he was even MORE determined to find a real, live giant squid!

He went on an expedition to islands in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean where sperm whales come to eat. The whales would show him the way!

Clyde knew he couldn't dive deep enough to follow the whales. Giant squids live much deeper than a scuba diver can go. So he needed a different plan.

He asked a photographer named Greg Marshall to help. Greg invented something called a "Crittercam"—a special video camera that can be attached to sea animals like dolphins, seals, and turtles. It records everything they see underwater!

Camera on a Whale

How do you put a camera on a 100,000-pound wild whale? Very carefully!

The researchers quietly paddled a small boat toward a whale resting just under the water. The whale stayed surprisingly calm. They stuck the camera to its head with a big suction cup. The camera also had recorders to measure how deep the whale went and what sounds it made.

An hour later, the camera fell off the whale. It floated in the water, making a beeping sound. The scientists found it and pulled it onto their boat.

They were so excited! They played the video right away. Would they finally see a giant squid?

🎤 What They Heard

The whale had gone down 2,600 feet—that's deeper than 8 football fields stacked up! The camera lights could only shine a few feet in that darkness.

But the sound recorder picked up something cool! They could hear other whales calling to each other. Maybe they were saying, "Hey! I found a great place to get squid!"

Clyde's team put the camera on whales seven more times. He learned a lot about where giant squids live and what eats them. But he still hadn't seen a giant squid.

🤖 Robot Submarines 🤖

Exploring the deep ocean with glowing lights

Another scientist told Clyde that he found a certain kind of fish called orange roughy inside dead giant squids. So Clyde went to an underwater canyon where fishermen catch these fish. Sperm whales go there too.

Clyde used a special underwater robot controlled by computer. It was like he was swimming with the fish as he watched from the control room!

The Deep Rover

On Clyde's next trip, he used a small submarine called the Deep Rover. It explored an underwater canyon near New Zealand where another favorite food of giant squids lives—hoki fish. Young sperm whales go there to eat giant squids!

Clyde thought that giant squids might be attracted to glowing light, like the light that many deep-sea animals make. So he covered the submarine with glow sticks—like the ones kids carry on Halloween!

A pilot sat inside the tiny submarine and drove it around. Cameras sent pictures back to Clyde in the ship's control room. He watched very carefully.

The team saw an amazing light show from many deep-sea creatures glowing blue and green. But the giant squid stayed hidden.

🍽️ Would You Eat Giant Squid?

Clyde LOVES to eat squid! (When it's cooked, squid is called calamari.) No one had ever tasted giant squid before. So Clyde, who's always curious, decided to try some.

"It was very, very bitter," he said. Scientists found out that giant squid meat has a chemical in it that's like something in our pee! Yuck! 🤢

However, with each trip, Clyde learned new things about the animals that eat giant squids and the animals that giant squids eat. All of these discoveries gave him more and more clues!

📸 Taking the Bait! 📸

Dr. Kubodera's brilliant idea!

A Japanese scientist named Dr. Tsunemi Kubodera kept searching. He used Clyde's research that showed sperm whales lead to giant squids.

Dr. Kubodera studied an area near Japan where sperm whales come to eat every year from September to December. He attached a camera to a long fishing line. The camera was programmed to take a picture every 30 seconds. He put chopped shrimp and squid on hooks to attract a giant squid. Then they lowered the line deep into the ocean and waited... and waited.

Dr. Kubodera went on 23 trips over three years. Finally, a giant squid grabbed the bait!

⏰ How Long Did It Fight?

The giant squid was caught on the line. How long do you think it fought before breaking free?

For four hours, the squid twisted and turned on the line. Sometimes it attacked the bait. Other times it tried to escape.

The camera took 556 pictures! Finally, the squid broke free. But it left behind one of its tentacles stuck on the hook. The tentacle was 18 feet long!

When the team brought the tentacle onto the boat, the big suckers grabbed onto the deck. Then they grabbed onto Dr. Kubodera's fingers!

🎉 First Photos and Video! 🎉

The first photos of a living giant squid!

These were the first photographs ever taken of a living giant squid! They were shown to the world in 2005. Clyde celebrated along with all the other scientists who loved squids!

Looking at the photos, Clyde saw something surprising. The giant squid was much more active than anyone expected! Despite its huge size, it wasn't slow and lazy. It was fast and strong! It made Clyde remember his encounter with the aggressive Humboldt squid.

🎥 And Then... VIDEO!

Two years later, in 2007, Dr. Kubodera's team did something even more amazing. They recorded the first VIDEO of a live giant squid!

They caught the squid at a depth of 2,133 feet and pulled it up to the surface. As they worked to bring it onto the boat, the squid fought hard! It thrashed around and shot powerful jets of water.

These observations proved that the giant squid is:

  • ⚡ A strong swimmer
  • 🎯 An active hunter
  • 💪 A fierce fighter

🔬 The Hunt Continues 🔬

Dr. Roper visiting the giant squid display

We have learned so much about ocean life since Clyde was a little boy tracking snails on the beach. He and other scientists have discovered a lot about giant squids and other ocean mysteries.

📚 What We Know Now:

✓ What giant squids eat

✓ What eats them

✓ More about how to find them

✓ More about their home in the deep ocean

✓ What they look like when they're alive and active

But there is SO much more we need to learn about this legendary creature! We still know very little about how it acts and how its body works.

Questions Still to Answer:

  • Do giant squids gather in big groups to find mates?
  • Do mother giant squids protect their eggs?
  • Do they squirt ink? Does the ink glow?
  • How strong are their arms and tentacles?

Scientists hope that someday they'll see one of the millions of giant squids swimming, eating, and just living its life in the dark, deep ocean.

Sea Monsters Are Real

Clyde Roper often visits the ocean hall in the Museum of Natural History. Two giant squids—a male and a female—are on display there. They float in special liquid inside clear cases so everyone can see them.

"Sea monsters are real," Clyde says, "and I have seen them." 🦑

🌊 Maybe YOU Will Help!

Scientists will keep learning about giant squids. And maybe someday, YOU will help discover the secrets of the giant squid!

Who knows? Maybe you'll be the one to finally see a giant squid swimming freely in its home, deep in the ocean! 🔬✨
