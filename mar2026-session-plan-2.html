Phase 4 • 0:22 - 0:46
Dictation-First Writing — How It Works
24 minutes • New approach: Talk → Find → Build → Check
Introduce the New Approach
"Here's what's different today: instead of looking at the frame and trying to fill in blanks, you're going to TALK first. Just tell me what you think — like you're explaining it to a friend. I'll write down what you say. Then we'll find the sentence hiding inside your words and build it together."
The 4-Step Protocol
1
Tell Me
Marcello talks. Tutor transcribes verbatim. Don't interrupt — let him talk as long as he wants.
2
Find the Sentence
Show him his own words. Read them back. "Can you find ONE sentence hiding in here?"
3
Build It
Use a LABELED frame based on what he said. The labels ([who], [what], [what happens]) tell him what KIND of word goes there.
4
Check
Read it back with speechSynthesis. "Does that sound like something you would say to a friend?"
Why this works: His oral narrative is STRONG — he performed a long coherent monologue about space travel on Mar 9 when given a source passage. The bottleneck is plan-to-sentence transfer, not ideas. Dictation-first lets his oral language be the raw material.

Reference
Differentiation + Troubleshooting
Quick reference for adjustments

If Short Response in "Tell Me"

  • "Tell me more. What does that look like?"
  • "If I was at your school, what would I SEE at recess?"
  • "Pretend you're the teacher. Explain it to ME."

If He Can't Find the Sentence

  • Underline the key phrase for him: "Look — you said THIS part. That's your sentence right there."
  • Read two chunks and ask: "Which one sounds more like an essay sentence?"

If Labeled Frames Are Still Too Hard

  • Pre-fill more: "I think schools should have longer recess because kids ___[what happens]___."
  • Offer 3 options based on what he said, let him pick
  • Last resort: tutor writes the sentence from his dictation, he reads it and confirms: "Is that what you meant?"

If He Goes on a Long Monologue

LET HIM. This is the raw material. The monologue is a feature, not a problem.
  • Let him finish. Don't interrupt.
  • Then: "That was AMAZING. Now let's find the ONE sentence hiding in that story."

If "New Things" / "Important" Pattern Appears

  • "You said 'new things' — what ARE the things? Name one."
  • "You said 'important' — important means it MATTERS. WHY does it matter? What would happen WITHOUT recess?"
